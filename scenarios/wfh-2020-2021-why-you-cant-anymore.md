# "I worked from home in 2020–2021 — can I still claim?"

**Short answer: no** (with one narrow out-of-state historical exception). A consuming agent
should deliver this cleanly rather than walking someone through a doomed filing.

## Tax year 2020 — fails twice

1. **On the merits:** [HB 197 §29](../law/hb-197-2020.md) deemed pandemic WFH days to be office
   days, and the Ohio Supreme Court upheld it in
   [Schaad v. Alder](../law/schaad-v-alder.md) (2024-Ohio-525, decided 2024-02-14). Ohio
   residents' 2020 office-city withholding was lawful and non-refundable. RITA held timely 2020
   claims "SUSPENDED" during the litigation; *Schaad* resolved them against claimants.
2. **On the clock:** the [3-year window](../concepts/statute-of-limitations.md) for 2020 closed
   around May 2024 anyway.

*The exception that proves the rule:* out-of-state residents — like the Pennsylvania plaintiff
in *Morsy v. Dumas*, taxed by Cleveland for days worked entirely in PA — did win refunds,
because Ohio can't authorize extraterritorial taxation beyond its borders. That path was
litigation, not Form 10A, and its window is likewise past.

## Tax year 2021 — was claimable, now expired

[HB 110](../law/hb-110-2021.md) let employers keep withholding to the office city through 2021
but required municipalities to refund employees for days not actually worked there. Workers who
filed a 2021 Form 10A got paid. The window closed **~April 18, 2025** (three years after the
2021 return deadline). A claim filed today would be denied as untimely.

## What to brief instead

The same person almost certainly has **open claims for 2023, 2024, and 2025** if the hybrid
pattern continued — see [Open claim windows](../concepts/open-claim-windows.md) and the
[flagship scenario](wfh-2024-medina-resident-solon-office.md). The pandemic years are the lesson;
the current years are the money.
