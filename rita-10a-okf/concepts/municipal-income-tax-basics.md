# Ohio municipal income tax basics

Ohio is one of the few states where **cities and villages levy their own income tax** on wages
and net profits. The state sets uniform ground rules in **Ohio Revised Code Chapter 718**, but
each municipal corporation sets its own rate (commonly 1%–3%) and its own credit policy.

Two facts about you decide who can tax a paycheck:

- **Where you live** (residence municipality): taxes residents on all income wherever earned,
  usually with some [credit for taxes paid](credit-for-taxes-paid.md) to a workplace city.
- **Where the work is physically performed** (workplace municipality): taxes wages earned inside
  its borders, and employers generally must withhold for it (ORC 718.03), subject to the
  [20-day occasional entrant rule](twenty-day-occasional-entrant.md).

**RITA (Regional Income Tax Agency)** is a council of governments that administers this tax for
roughly 350 member municipalities, heavily concentrated in Northeast Ohio — it processes their
returns, withholding, and refunds (including [Form 10A](../procedures/form-10a-overview.md)).
RITA is an administrator, not a taxing authority: the tax is always a specific municipality's.
Not every city uses RITA — see [RITA vs. CCA vs. self-administered](rita-vs-cca-vs-self-administered.md)
(notably, **Cleveland is not RITA**) and
[Townships and unincorporated areas](townships-and-unincorporated.md) (townships levy no income tax).

When an employer withholds for a city whose tax you don't actually owe — most commonly because
you worked from home — the withheld money isn't automatically returned. It must be claimed, on
**Form 10A**, within the [statute of limitations](statute-of-limitations.md).
