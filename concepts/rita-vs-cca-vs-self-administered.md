# RITA vs. CCA vs. self-administered cities

Ohio municipalities administer their income tax three ways, and **the refund paperwork follows
the administrator, not the taxpayer**:

1. **RITA members** (~350 municipalities) — file with RITA; refunds via RITA's
   [Form 10A](../procedures/form-10a-overview.md). Member list and rates:
   ritaohio.com/municipalities and the [municipalities/](../municipalities/index.md) nodes here.
2. **CCA members** — the Central Collection Agency, run by the City of Cleveland's Division of
   Taxation. **Cleveland itself is CCA, not RITA** (Cleveland: 2.50% rate, 100% credit, 2.50%
   credit limit, effective 2017). CCA has its own refund forms and procedures — this bundle
   covers the boundary but not CCA procedure. See ccaohio.gov.
3. **Self-administered cities** — run their own tax departments and are in *neither* agency.
   Verified examples (2026): **Brunswick** (2.0%), **Parma** (2.5%), **Lakewood** (1.5%; CCA
   handles some employer-withholding relationships as a "special member"), **Fairlawn** (2%).
   Refund claims go directly to the city's own tax department on its own forms.

## Why this matters

- The classic scenario — *live in a RITA suburb, work in Cleveland* — spans **two agencies**.
  Refunds of Cleveland withholding are CCA's, not RITA's; the residence-side consequences are
  RITA's. See the scenario node
  [Cleveland office, RITA suburb](../scenarios/cleveland-office-cca-boundary.md).
- Filing RITA's 10A for a non-RITA city's tax accomplishes nothing — RITA cannot refund tax it
  doesn't administer. First question in any refund conversation: **who administers each
  municipality involved?** If a city is not in [municipalities/](../municipalities/index.md),
  check RITA's member list, then CCA's, then the city's own website.
